GST Services and Information

Introduction to GST

Goods and Services Tax (GST) is a comprehensive indirect tax levied on the supply of goods and services across India. It was implemented on July 1, 2017, marking a significant reform in India's indirect taxation system. The primary objective of GST was to subsume a multitude of existing indirect taxes such as VAT, Service Tax, Central Excise Duty, Entry Tax, Octroi, etc., into a single, unified tax structure. This consolidation aimed to simplify the tax regime, reduce cascading effects (tax on tax), enhance transparency, and improve overall economic efficiency.

At its core, GST is a destination-based consumption tax. This means that the tax is levied at the point of consumption, and the revenue accrues to the state where the goods or services are consumed, rather than the state where they are produced. It is also a value-added tax (VAT) system, where tax is collected at each stage of the supply chain on the value added at that particular stage. Businesses can typically claim input tax credit (ITC) for the GST paid on their purchases, which helps in avoiding the cascading effect of taxes and ensures that the tax burden is ultimately borne by the final consumer.

The GST framework in India operates under a dual model, where both the Central and State governments have the power to levy and collect taxes. This leads to different components of GST:

  • CGST (Central Goods and Services Tax): Collected by the Central Government on intra-state supplies of goods and services (i.e., transactions within the same state).
  • SGST (State Goods and Services Tax): Collected by the State Governments on intra-state supplies of goods and services. For Union Territories with their own legislature, this is referred to as SGST.
  • IGST (Integrated Goods and Services Tax): Collected by the Central Government on inter-state supplies of goods and services (i.e., transactions between two different states or a state and a Union Territory) and also on imports. The revenue from IGST is then apportioned between the Centre and the consuming State.
  • UTGST (Union Territory Goods and Services Tax): Collected by the Union Territory administration on intra-UT supplies of goods and services in Union Territories without their own legislature (e.g., Chandigarh, Andaman & Nicobar Islands). This is levied in lieu of SGST.

This multi-faceted tax structure is designed to ensure a seamless flow of credit and a more straightforward taxation system for businesses operating across India, contributing to the vision of 'One Nation, One Tax, One Market'.

GST Registration

GST registration is the process by which a taxpayer gets registered under the Goods and Services Tax (GST) regime. It is mandatory for businesses and individuals who meet certain criteria. The primary criterion for GST registration is the aggregate turnover. Generally, any supplier of goods or services or both whose aggregate turnover in a financial year exceeds a specified threshold limit is liable to be registered under GST. For most states, this threshold is ₹20 lakhs (₹10 lakhs for Special Category States like those in the North-East and hilly regions). However, for suppliers exclusively engaged in the supply of goods, the threshold limit is ₹40 lakhs for most states, subject to certain conditions.

Beyond turnover-based liability, there are several cases where GST registration is mandatory irrespective of turnover. These include persons making any inter-state taxable supply, casual taxable persons, persons who are required to pay tax under reverse charge, non-resident taxable persons, persons who are required to deduct tax at source (TDS), e-commerce operators, and suppliers of goods through e-commerce operators who are required to collect tax at source (TCS). Input Service Distributors (ISD) and those supplying online information and database access or retrieval services (OIDAR) from a place outside India to a person in India, other than a registered taxable person, also require mandatory registration.

Obtaining GST registration offers several benefits to a business. Firstly, the business gets recognized as a legally registered supplier of goods or services. This allows them to legally collect GST from their customers and pass on the credit of the taxes paid on the goods or services supplied to the purchasers/recipients. Registered businesses can also claim Input Tax Credit (ITC) on their own purchases and input services, which reduces the cost of goods/services and makes the business more competitive. Furthermore, GST registration is often a prerequisite for various business dealings, such as participating in tenders or supplying to larger corporations.

The process for GST registration is entirely online through the common GST portal maintained by the Goods and Services Tax Network (GSTN). Applicants need to submit necessary documents, which typically include the PAN card of the applicant, proof of constitution of business (e.g., Partnership Deed, Certificate of Incorporation), identity and address proof of promoters/directors with photographs, proof of principal place of business (e.g., rent agreement, electricity bill), and bank account details (e.g., a copy of a cancelled cheque or bank statement). Once the application is submitted along with the required documents, the tax authorities verify the application and grant the GST registration certificate (Form GST REG-06) if everything is in order.

GST Returns (GSTR-1, GSTR-3B, etc.)

GST returns are official documents that registered taxpayers must file with the tax authorities, detailing their income, expenses, and other relevant tax information. Filing GST returns is a critical aspect of compliance under the GST regime. These returns are the primary mechanism for taxpayers to declare their tax liabilities, claim Input Tax Credit (ITC), and provide information about their transactions. Accurate and timely filing of GST returns is essential for the seamless flow of credit in the GST system and for maintaining a good compliance rating.

There are various types of GST returns prescribed under the GST law, each serving a specific purpose and applicable to different categories of taxpayers. Some of the most common returns include:

  • GSTR-1: This is a monthly or quarterly return (depending on turnover) for declaring details of all outward supplies of goods and services made by the taxpayer. It includes invoice-wise details of sales to registered taxpayers and consolidated details for sales to consumers.
  • GSTR-3B: This is a monthly self-declaration and summary return that taxpayers file to report their summarized GST liabilities, input tax credit claimed, and tax paid. It is mandatory for all normal registered taxpayers.
  • GSTR-9: This is an annual return that must be filed once a year by registered taxpayers under GST. It is a consolidated summary of all monthly/quarterly returns (GSTR-1, GSTR-2A/2B, GSTR-3B) filed during that financial year. It includes details of outward supplies, inward supplies, taxes paid, ITC claimed, refunds claimed, and demands raised, etc.
  • GSTR-9C: This is a reconciliation statement to be filed annually by taxpayers whose aggregate turnover exceeds a specified limit (currently ₹5 crore). It needs to be certified by a Chartered Accountant or Cost Accountant and reconciles the figures reported in the audited annual financial statements with those reported in the GSTR-9.

The frequency of filing for most returns like GSTR-1 and GSTR-3B is typically monthly. However, taxpayers with an aggregate turnover up to ₹5 crore in the preceding financial year have the option to file GSTR-1 on a quarterly basis under the QRMP (Quarterly Return Monthly Payment) scheme, though they still need to pay tax monthly through a simple challan. GSTR-9 and GSTR-9C are annual returns.

Late or non-filing of GST returns can lead to significant consequences, including the levy of late fees and interest. The late fee is prescribed per day of delay for each return, subject to a maximum cap. Persistent non-filing can also lead to other penal actions, such as suspension or cancellation of GST registration and blocking of e-way bill generation. Moreover, the timely filing of returns by suppliers is crucial for recipients to be able to claim Input Tax Credit, as the details of supplies appear in the recipient's GSTR-2A/2B based on the supplier's GSTR-1 filings.

GST Invoicing Rules

Proper invoicing is a cornerstone of the Goods and Services Tax (GST) system in India. A GST invoice is not just a bill for goods or services; it's a crucial document that serves as evidence of the supply and is essential for the recipient to claim Input Tax Credit (ITC). Adherence to GST invoicing rules is mandatory for all registered taxpayers, as it ensures transparency in transactions and helps in preventing tax evasion. The government has prescribed specific rules regarding the issuance of tax invoices, including the particulars they must contain and the manner in which they should be issued.

A valid GST invoice must contain several mandatory fields to be considered compliant. These include, but are not limited to: the supplier's name, address, and GST Identification Number (GSTIN); a consecutive serial number (unique for a financial year); date of issue; recipient's name, address, and GSTIN (if registered); HSN (Harmonized System of Nomenclature) code for goods or SAC (Services Accounting Code) for services; description of goods or services; quantity and unit; total value of supply; taxable value of supply; applicable rate of GST (CGST, SGST, IGST, UTGST, Cess); amount of tax charged; place of supply; and signature or digital signature of the supplier or their authorized representative. For inter-state supplies, the place of supply along with the name of the State is crucial.

Under GST, different types of invoices or documents are prescribed for different situations:

  • Tax Invoice: Issued by a registered taxpayer when making a taxable supply of goods or services. This is the primary document for the recipient to claim ITC.
  • Bill of Supply: Issued by a registered taxpayer who is supplying exempted goods or services, or by a taxpayer who has opted for the composition scheme. A Bill of Supply is issued instead of a Tax Invoice in these cases, and no tax is collected on it.
  • Debit Note: Issued by a supplier when the taxable value or tax charged in the original tax invoice is found to be less than what is due, or when goods supplied are returned by the recipient (if the original tax has already been paid by the supplier on such returned goods and the recipient has not yet availed ITC).
  • Credit Note: Issued by a supplier when the taxable value or tax charged in the original tax invoice is found to exceed what is due, or when goods supplied are returned by the recipient, or where services are found to be deficient.

Furthermore, India has been progressively implementing 'e-invoicing' (electronic invoicing) for B2B (Business to Business) transactions. Under the e-invoicing system, taxpayers above a certain turnover threshold must generate invoices on their internal systems and then report them online to the Invoice Registration Portal (IRP). The IRP validates the information and returns the digitally signed e-invoice with a unique Invoice Reference Number (IRN) and a QR code. E-invoicing aims to standardize invoice formats, reduce data entry errors, enable real-time tracking of invoices, and curb tax evasion by ensuring that invoices are reported to the GST system before the transaction takes place or shortly thereafter.

GST Audits and Assessments

GST audit is the examination of records, returns, and other documents maintained or furnished by a registered person under the GST laws. The purpose of a GST audit is to verify the correctness of turnover declared, taxes paid, refund claimed, and input tax credit availed, and to assess the compliance with the provisions of GST. Audits play a crucial role in ensuring tax compliance and preventing tax evasion, thereby safeguarding government revenue. They help in identifying discrepancies and ensuring that businesses adhere to the complex web of GST regulations.

There are primarily three types of audits envisaged under the GST regime:

  • Audit by CA/CMA (Section 35(5) - Now Omitted/Modified): Initially, every registered person whose aggregate turnover during a financial year exceeded ₹2 crore was required to get their accounts audited by a Chartered Accountant (CA) or Cost Accountant (CMA) and submit a copy of the audited annual accounts, the reconciliation statement (GSTR-9C), and other prescribed documents. However, this requirement for audit by CA/CMA has been modified. While GSTR-9C (reconciliation statement) is still required for taxpayers with turnover above ₹5 crore, it can now be self-certified by the taxpayer. Direct audit by a CA/CMA mandated by statute for all above a certain threshold has been removed, but businesses may still opt for internal audits or audits for their own assurance.
  • Audit by Tax Authorities (Departmental Audit - Section 65): The Commissioner or any officer authorized by him can undertake an audit of any registered person for such period, at such frequency, and in such manner as may be prescribed. These audits are conducted by the GST department itself. The taxpayer is given prior notice, and the audit is typically conducted at the taxpayer's place of business or in the office of the tax authorities. The objective is to verify compliance with GST provisions.
  • Special Audit (Section 66): If at any stage of scrutiny, inquiry, investigation, or any other proceedings before him, any officer not below the rank of Assistant Commissioner, having regard to the nature and complexity of the case and the interest of revenue, is of the opinion that the value has not been correctly declared or the credit availed is not within the normal limits, he may, with the prior approval of the Commissioner, direct such registered person by a communication in writing to get his records including books of account examined and audited by a Chartered Accountant or a Cost Accountant as may be nominated by the Commissioner. This is a more focused audit ordered by the department when specific issues are identified.

During an audit, if discrepancies such as non-payment or short payment of tax, incorrect availment of ITC, or erroneous refunds are detected, the tax authorities will initiate an assessment process. This involves issuing a show-cause notice to the taxpayer, providing an opportunity to be heard, and then passing an adjudication order quantifying the tax, interest, and penalty payable. Taxpayers have the right to appeal against such orders if they disagree with the findings of the audit or the assessment made by the authorities.

Assessments under GST can also be self-assessments (where the taxpayer themselves assesses their liability and pays tax), provisional assessments (when the taxpayer is unable to determine value or rate), scrutiny assessments (based on scrutiny of returns), and best judgment assessments (when a taxpayer fails to file returns). These processes are designed to ensure that the correct amount of tax is collected and deposited with the government.

LUT Filing for Exporters

A Letter of Undertaking (LUT) under GST is a document that exporters can file to export goods or services without having to pay Integrated Goods and Services Tax (IGST) upfront. Normally, IGST is applicable on all exports. However, the GST law provides two options for exporters: either pay IGST on exports and then claim a refund of the tax paid, or export under an LUT without paying IGST and then claim a refund of the accumulated Input Tax Credit (ITC) on inputs/input services used in making those exports.

The primary benefit of filing an LUT is that it allows exporters to conduct their export business without the burden of paying IGST at the time of export. This significantly helps in managing working capital, as funds are not blocked in tax payments that would later be claimed as a refund. This facility is particularly beneficial for businesses that have substantial export volumes, as it streamlines their financial operations and improves cash flow. It simplifies the export process by removing the step of tax payment and subsequent refund claim for the tax amount itself (though ITC refund claims might still be necessary).

Any registered taxpayer who is exporting goods or services can file an LUT, provided they have not been prosecuted for any offence under the CGST Act or the Integrated Goods and Services Tax Act, 2017, or any of the existing laws in force in a case where the amount of tax evaded exceeds two hundred and fifty lakh rupees. Essentially, it is available to most exporters who intend to supply goods or services for export without payment of integrated tax. This includes all SEZ units and SEZ developers as well. If an exporter is not eligible to file an LUT (e.g., due to past prosecution), they would have to export by paying IGST and then claim a refund.

The Letter of Undertaking is filed online using Form GST RFD-11 on the GST portal. It needs to be furnished before undertaking the export. Once filed and accepted, an LUT is generally valid for the entire financial year in which it is submitted. This means an exporter can make multiple export shipments during the financial year under the same LUT. It is important for exporters to comply with the conditions of the LUT, such as ensuring that the export proceeds are realized within the time frame prescribed under the Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA). Failure to comply with these conditions can lead to the revocation of the LUT facility, and the exporter may then be required to pay IGST on subsequent exports.

E-Way Bill System

The E-Way Bill (Electronic Way Bill) is a mandatory electronic document required for the movement of goods under the GST regime in India. Its primary purpose is to track the movement of goods and ensure that they are transported in compliance with GST laws, thereby preventing tax evasion. It integrates information related to the goods being transported, the consignor, the consignee, and the transporter, providing a digital trail for tax authorities.

An E-Way Bill must be generated before the commencement of movement of goods when the value of the consignment exceeds a specified threshold, which is generally ₹50,000. This requirement applies to movements for any reason, including supply, reasons other than supply (like returns, job work, branch transfers), or inward supply from an unregistered person. It is required for both inter-state and intra-state movements, although states may have their own specific rules regarding intra-state E-Way Bill generation, including different threshold limits or exemptions for certain goods.

The responsibility for generating the E-Way Bill typically lies with the person causing the movement of goods. This could be the consignor (supplier) or the consignee (recipient) if they are registered under GST. If the movement is initiated by an unregistered person, the transporter must generate the E-Way Bill. The E-Way Bill can be generated on the common GST E-Way Bill portal. The person generating the E-Way Bill needs to provide details of the invoice or bill of supply/challan, transporter details (like Transporter ID and vehicle number if transported by road), and details of the goods (HSN code, quantity, taxable value, tax rate).

Once generated, the E-Way Bill has a specific validity period, which depends on the distance the goods are to be transported. For every 200 kilometers or part thereof, the validity is one day for regular vehicles/cargo. For over-dimensional cargo, the validity is one day for every 20 kilometers or part thereof. The E-Way Bill can be extended under certain exceptional circumstances. It is crucial for the person in charge of the conveyance to carry the E-Way Bill (either physically or in electronic form) along with the invoice/bill of supply/delivery challan during the movement of goods. Non-compliance can lead to detention of the vehicle and goods, along with penalties.

GST Compliance and Due Dates

Ongoing GST compliance is paramount for all registered businesses in India to avoid legal repercussions and ensure smooth operations. The GST regime is built on a foundation of self-assessment and regular reporting, making it crucial for taxpayers to stay updated with their obligations. Effective GST compliance involves a series of activities that must be performed accurately and within stipulated timelines. This not only helps businesses meet their legal duties but also contributes to the overall efficiency and transparency of the taxation system.

Key GST compliance activities include, but are not limited to:

  • Timely Filing of GST Returns: Submitting various returns like GSTR-1, GSTR-3B, and annual returns (GSTR-9/9C) by their respective due dates.
  • Correct GST Invoicing: Issuing invoices that are compliant with GST rules, including all mandatory particulars and correct HSN/SAC codes. This also includes adherence to e-invoicing regulations where applicable.
  • Accurate Payment of Taxes: Calculating the correct GST liability (output tax less eligible input tax credit) and paying it to the government within the prescribed due dates.
  • Input Tax Credit (ITC) Reconciliation: Regularly reconciling ITC claimed in returns with the details available in GSTR-2A/2B to ensure accuracy and avoid future disputes.
  • Maintenance of Records: Proper upkeep of all GST-related records, including invoices, bills of supply, debit/credit notes, payment challans, ITC registers, and stock registers for the prescribed period (currently at least six years from the due date of filing the annual return for the year pertaining to such accounts and records).
  • E-Way Bill Generation: Ensuring E-Way Bills are generated for the movement of goods as per the rules.
  • Responding to Notices: Timely and appropriate responses to any notices or communications received from the GST authorities.

Adherence to due dates is a critical aspect of GST compliance. While specific due dates for various returns and payments are notified by the government and can sometimes be extended, businesses must diligently track these dates. For instance, GSTR-3B is typically due on the 20th, 22nd, or 24th of the following month, depending on the taxpayer's state and turnover. GSTR-1 due dates depend on whether the taxpayer files monthly or quarterly. It is advisable to refer to the official GST portal or consult with a tax professional for the latest due dates and any changes announced by the CBIC (Central Board of Indirect Taxes and Customs).

Non-compliance with GST provisions can lead to various adverse consequences. These include the levy of late fees for delayed return filing, interest on delayed payment of taxes, penalties for specific contraventions (e.g., incorrect invoicing, failure to register), and in severe cases, prosecution. Furthermore, non-compliance can result in the suspension or cancellation of GST registration, disruption of business operations (e.g., inability to generate E-Way Bills), and loss of reputation. Therefore, maintaining a high level of GST compliance is essential for business continuity and growth.
